# Vision

## Mission

Build the definitive self-contained textbook for Stanford CS336: Language Modeling from Scratch. The book serves as both a companion to the course and a standalone resource for any CS undergraduate who wants to deeply understand how modern language models work — from information theory through alignment.

## Guiding Principles

### 1. "From Scratch" Philosophy
Like CS336 itself, this book builds everything from first principles. We do not hand-wave over derivations, skip implementation details, or expect the reader to "just trust" a formula. Every major result is derived, explained, and connected to code.

### 2. Mathematical Rigor Without Gatekeeping
Every definition is precise. Every assumption is stated. But we never weaponize notation — every symbol is introduced with a plain-English explanation, every equation gets a prose interpretation, and every derivation is accompanied by a worked example.

### 3. Self-Contained Prerequisites
We do not assume the reader knows NLP, linguistics, CUDA, or distributed systems. Part 0 provides the exact prerequisite knowledge needed, and inline "prerequisite capsules" fill gaps throughout.

### 4. Neuroscience-Grounded Intuition
Most analogies come from brain science. But we are honest: every analogy box states what is similar, what is different, and what intuition the reader should keep. We never imply that LLMs are brains.

### 5. Primary-Source Grounded
Claims are backed by the original papers, official technical reports, and official course materials. We prefer primary citations over blog posts or secondary summaries.

### 6. Course-Aligned but Not Course-Limited
The narrative spine follows CS336's lecture arc and assignment structure. But the book adds prerequisite coverage, deeper derivations, and more worked examples than the lectures provide.

## Target Audience

A CS undergraduate (junior/senior level) who:
- Can program fluently in Python
- Has used AI tools (ChatGPT, Copilot) or done "vibe coding"
- May have trained or fine-tuned small models
- Has taken intro ML and linear algebra courses
- Does **not** deeply know NLP, linguistics, CUDA, or distributed training

## Success Criteria

1. A student can follow the book from Chapter 0 through Chapter 26 without consulting another textbook
2. Every chapter compiles in LaTeX without errors
3. Every claim has a citation to a primary source
4. Every neuroscience analogy follows the three-section format
5. The CS336 coverage matrix shows 100% coverage for Spring 2025
6. At least 80% of publicly available Spring 2026 topics are addressed or noted
